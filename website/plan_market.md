## Key Findings

**$9.99 is underpriced for this market.**
The one-time purchase segment clusters between **$29–$79** for solo use.
Timemator at $39 is the most comparable product (CSV import, local data, overtime tracking) and has strong reviews at that price.
Pricing at $9.99 anchors CountMyHours as a throw-away utility rather than a professional tool, even though the features — extras analysis, sold-hour tracking, 9 languages, holiday calendars — are arguably more complete.

**"Pay once, own forever" is a genuine differentiator.**
Chronoid built its entire brand around this and prices confidently at $49–$99.
Users specifically search for "no subscription time tracking" to escape $100+/year tools.

**Gumroad economics favour direct sales.**
Gumroad takes ~10%; Apple App Store takes 30%.
On a $29.99 sale: Gumroad nets ~$27, App Store nets ~$21.
Direct Windows distribution via Gumroad is the right call.

**Windows is whitespace.**
Most premium indie time-tracking apps (Timemator, Chronoid, Daily, Timing) are **macOS only**.
ManicTime is the main cross-platform player.
A polished Windows version at a competitive price has real room to grow.

---

## Pricing Recommendation

| Platform | Suggested price | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| macOS (Gumroad or App Store) | **$19.99** | Undercuts Timemator ($39) while signalling real value; clear "professional tool" price point |
| Windows (Gumroad) | **$19.99** | Parity with macOS; simpler buyer decision |
| Both platforms bundle | **$29.99** | Incentivises cross-platform purchase; strong perceived value |

$19.99 is the sweet spot:
- Half of what Timemator charges
- A fraction of what Timing costs in a year ($120+)
- Clearly in "professional tool" territory, not "cheap download"
